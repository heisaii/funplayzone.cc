# Every Official World Cup Song Ranked, From "Notti Magiche" to "Waka Waka"

*From Ricky Martin's brass-blasted Latin-pop coronation to Shakira's immortal stadium spell, the official World Cup song canon reveals one brutal truth: football anthems only matter when fans steal them from FIFA.*

## Before the Ranking: The World Cup Anthem Is Pop's Most Dangerous Assignment

A World Cup song is not really a song. It is a commercial, a prayer, a tourist brochure, a halftime cue, a soft-power handshake, and **a karaoke trap for billions of people who may not share a language but suddenly share blood pressure.**

That is why ranking them is both easy and impossible. The weak ones expose themselves quickly: too polished, too committee-shaped, too desperate to say "unity" without creating any. **The great ones behave differently — they don't simply promote the tournament. They become part of how people remember it.**

The modern official-song tradition begins in Italy 1990, when FIFA adopted official tournament music including *"Un'estate italiana (Notti magiche)"* and Giorgio Moroder Project's *"To Be Number One."* FIFA's own historical framing then traces the single-song canon through *"Gloryland"* in 1994, *"La Copa de la Vida"* in 1998, *"Boom"* in 2002, *"The Time of Our Lives"* in 2006, *"Waka Waka"* in 2010, *"We Are One"* in 2014, and *"Live It Up"* in 2018.

Qatar 2022 complicated the formula by moving into a multi-song Official Soundtrack, with *"Hayya Hayya (Better Together)"* released as the first of several singles rather than a clean, single-anthem successor. So this ranking focuses on the official song era as a pop canon — the songs that tried, sometimes gloriously and sometimes disastrously, to compress the entire planet into one hook.

> **The rule is simple: the best World Cup songs do not sound global because they mention the world. They sound global because the world decides to sing them badly.**
>
>

## 8. "Boom" — Anastacia, 2002

There is a certain charm to how aggressively *"Boom"* misunderstands the assignment. Anastacia attacks the song like she is trying to **win the World Cup personally**, belting through a slab of early-2000s pop-rock that feels more like a sports drink commercial than a planetary ritual.

It is not boring, which already saves it from true failure. But it is **too vertical, too clenched** — too interested in impact and not interested enough in release. A World Cup song needs muscle, yes, but it also needs air. *"Boom"* has the punch; it forgets the crowd.

## 7. "Live It Up" — Nicky Jam, Will Smith & Era Istrefi, 2018

On paper, *"Live It Up"* has all the ingredients: reggaeton momentum, Hollywood familiarity, festival-pop gloss, and Diplo's instinct for frictionless scale. But that is exactly the problem. **It sounds like the answer to a brand brief that asked for "global energy" and got exactly that — nothing less, but nothing more.**

Guinness World Records notes that the original version had **152,509,250 Spotify streams**, far behind *"Waka Waka"* — which is not a perfect measure of cultural value, but does capture the emotional gap.

It moves, but it never feels possessed.

## 6. "The Time of Our Lives" — Il Divo featuring Toni Braxton, 2006

This is the most dignified entry in the canon — which is another way of saying **it is not quite built for football chaos.**

*"The Time of Our Lives"* treats the World Cup like an Olympic gala: sweeping, formal, handsomely lit, and slightly afraid of sweat. Il Divo gives the song operatic lift; Toni Braxton gives it human gravity. The result is elegant, but **elegance is not always what this job requires.**

A World Cup anthem has to survive beer, tears, horns, bad speakers, and fans screaming the wrong words. This one belongs to the ceremony, not the street. It is beautiful. It is also **too well-behaved to become immortal.**

## 5. "We Are One (Ole Ola)" — Pitbull, Jennifer Lopez & Claudia Leitte, 2014

There is a version of *"We Are One"* that is easy to mock: Pitbull doing motivational-corporate Pitbull things, Jennifer Lopez arriving like **a halftime show inside another halftime show**, and Claudia Leitte carrying the burden of making Brazil feel present inside a song that often feels assembled elsewhere.

But dismissing it completely would be unfair. **The chorus is sticky. The percussion is bright.** The whole thing has the glossy shamelessness of a sponsored beach party that somehow becomes fun after the second drink.

Still, the numbers tell part of the story: Guinness lists **333,804,341 Spotify streams** across three versions — again far below the Shakira benchmark. It is not a great World Cup song. It is a very efficient one.

## 4. "Gloryland" — Daryl Hall & Sounds of Blackness, 1994

*"Gloryland"* is the strangest survivor here because it barely resembles the World Cup song as we understand it now. It does not chase the club, the chant, or the global-pop shortcut. Instead, **it leans into American gospel-pop earnestness**, trying to make USA 1994 sound less like a spectacle and more like a benediction.

That makes it dated, but not disposable. There is warmth in its sincerity, and Sounds of Blackness give the song **a communal depth that many later anthems try to fake with bigger drums and cheaper slogans.** It may not make you want to dance, but it understands that football is also a faith system.

## 3. "Un'estate italiana (Notti magiche)" — Edoardo Bennato & Gianna Nannini, 1990

The origin story still glows. *"Un'estate italiana"* — better known through its dream-lit phrase *"Notti magiche"* — is not the loudest World Cup song or the most globally engineered. It does not bulldoze you into celebration. **It rises slowly, romantically, almost naively**, as if the tournament were not a corporate machine but a summer hallucination.

That is its power. It sounds like **floodlights, old television color, late-night streets, and national hope before the penalty shootout ruins everyone.** Compared with the later era of maximal slogans, *"Notti magiche"* feels almost fragile — less a stadium anthem than a memory pretending to be a song.

## 2. "La Copa de la Vida / The Cup of Life" — Ricky Martin, 1998

Here is where the modern World Cup anthem truly learns to sweat. Ricky Martin's *"La Copa de la Vida"* does not politely invite you into the tournament; **it kicks the door open with horns, drums, hips, and absolute theatrical confidence.** FIFA notes that Martin performed the official France 1998 song at the Stade de France before the final, and that the track reached **No. 1 in more than 30 countries.**

Everything about it understands scale. The song is simple without being empty, explosive without becoming faceless, and camp without losing athletic force. **It is not subtle because subtlety would be cowardice here.** If *"Waka Waka"* is the World Cup anthem as shared memory, *"The Cup of Life"* is the World Cup anthem as conquest.

## 1. "Waka Waka (This Time for Africa)" — Shakira featuring Freshlyground, 2010

**The obvious winner is still the correct one.**

*"Waka Waka"* did what every official World Cup song dreams of doing: **it escaped.** It escaped FIFA, escaped South Africa 2010, escaped the closing credits, escaped the algorithm before the algorithm became the only weather system in pop. It became playground choreography, wedding music, football nostalgia, YouTube immortality, and **one of the last true monoculture sports songs.**

Guinness World Records lists it as the most-streamed FIFA World Cup song on Spotify, with **1,074,458,645 streams** counted by January 13, 2025. Its video had also passed **4 billion views on YouTube.**

But the statistics are not the reason it wins. **It wins because it feels physically remembered.** Shakira and Freshlyground made a song that could be shouted, danced, mispronounced, remixed, and emotionally overused without collapsing. It had color, rhythm, generosity, and just enough friction to feel alive.

> *"Waka Waka"* is not merely the best official World Cup song. **It is the song that made every official World Cup song after it sound like an attempt.**
>
>

## The Canon After the Canon

The problem with *"Waka Waka"* is that it became **too good at its job.** After 2010, the World Cup anthem stopped being just another ceremonial pop product. It became a standard, a ghost, a dare.

That is why the official song canon is so revealing. **The weakest entries try to sound global by smoothing themselves into nothing. The strongest ones do the opposite: they risk personality.** *"Notti magiche"* risks sentimentality. *"The Cup of Life"* risks flamboyance. *"Waka Waka"* risks joy so direct that cynicism has nowhere to stand.

With 2026 now bringing Shakira back alongside Burna Boy on *"Dai Dai,"* the question is not whether FIFA can commission another official song — it already has. **The question is whether any new anthem can still do what the old great ones did: become too loved, too annoying, too public, and too emotionally useful to remain official.**

A World Cup song only wins when it stops sounding like FIFA's property and **starts sounding like everybody's bad idea at once.**
