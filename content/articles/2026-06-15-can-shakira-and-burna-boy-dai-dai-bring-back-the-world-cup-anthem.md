# Can Shakira and Burna Boy's "Dai Dai" Bring Back the World Cup Anthem?

*As the 2026 World Cup kicks off, Shakira and Burna Boy's "Dai Dai" attempts to revive the fading ritual of the global football anthem—while standing directly under the enormous shadow of "Waka Waka."*

## **The Anthem That Arrived Carrying a Ghost**

The World Cup anthem is one of pop music's strangest machines. It is expected to sound like joy, nationalism, airport duty-free, childhood memory, halftime spectacle, and a beer commercial all at once. It has to be simple enough for a stadium and enormous enough for billions. It has to feel local, global, official, spontaneous, emotional, and endlessly reusable.

That is the absurd brief handed to Shakira and Burna Boy with **"Dai Dai," the Official FIFA World Cup 2026 Song**. FIFA announced the track on May 15, 2026, released via Sony Music Latin, with royalties supporting the FIFA Global Citizen Education Fund, which aims to raise **USD 100 million** by the end of the tournament for education and football opportunities for children.

The timing could not be heavier. The 2026 World Cup opens on **June 11, 2026**, in Mexico City and expands into the biggest edition in tournament history: **48 teams, 104 matches, 16 locations, and three host countries—Canada, Mexico, and the United States**.

In other words, **"Dai Dai" is not just a song. It is a stress test for whether the World Cup anthem still matters.**

## **The Impossible Job of Replacing a Memory**

Every new World Cup song has to compete with the tournament. "Dai Dai" has to compete with a ghost.

That ghost is **"Waka Waka (This Time for Africa),"** Shakira's 2010 collaboration with South African band Freshlyground. It is not merely a successful official song; it is the song that rewired what a modern World Cup anthem could be. Guinness World Records lists "Waka Waka" as the most-streamed FIFA World Cup song on Spotify, with **1,074,458,645 streams** counted by January 13, 2025. Its video had also passed **4 billion views** on YouTube.

Those numbers are impressive, but numbers are not why "Waka Waka" still feels untouchable. It survived because it became an emotional infrastructure. For millions of people, the song is not just attached to South Africa 2010; it is attached to school assemblies, street screenings, plastic horns, grainy YouTube uploads, first crushes, national heartbreak, and the illusion that the entire planet briefly knew the same choreography. **That is the cruel part of Shakira's return: she is not being asked to make a hit. She is being asked to recreate weather.**

## **Shakira Returns to the Scene of Her Own Mythology**

Bringing Shakira back is a brilliant move because no other pop star carries this much World Cup muscle memory. It is also a dangerous move because no other pop star carries this much World Cup baggage.

Her presence instantly tells casual listeners that "Dai Dai" is meant to be taken seriously as an anthem, not just another soundtrack placement. She is the closest thing FIFA has to a returning champion in pop form: instantly recognizable, physically associated with football ceremony, and still bound to the one World Cup song that even non-football fans can identify within seconds.

But nostalgia is a trap with very good lighting. The more "Dai Dai" benefits from Shakira's history, the more it becomes trapped inside it. Every beat, chant, hook, and stadium-sized gesture risks being heard as a referendum on 2010. **The problem is not that "Dai Dai" sounds after "Waka Waka." The problem is that it exists after "Waka Waka."**

No 2026 anthem can recreate the old internet, the old monoculture, or the old way pop traveled before every song became a short-form video prompt. "Dai Dai" enters a world where attention is fractured, fan culture is hyper-regional, and a track can trend everywhere without becoming a shared memory anywhere.

## **Burna Boy as the Anti-Nostalgia Engine**

This is why Burna Boy matters. Without him, "Dai Dai" could have felt like a pure nostalgia rescue mission: call Shakira, summon the old magic, hope the algorithm mistakes memory for momentum.

Instead, Burna Boy gives the song a present tense.

The Recording Academy lists him with **one Grammy win and 13 nominations** and describes him as one of the defining global voices of Afrobeats, blending African rhythms, dancehall, reggae, hip-hop, and R&B into a sound that has helped expand African music's international reach.

That makes his presence more than decorative. It prevents "Dai Dai" from becoming a museum piece with a fresh logo. Shakira brings World Cup mythology; Burna Boy brings the current map of global pop. Together, they represent the exact fantasy FIFA wants to project in 2026: Latin pop memory, African pop momentum, and a North American mega-tournament trying to speak to everyone at once.

It is almost too perfect as branding. **The challenge is making that perfection feel less like a campaign deck and more like a crowd losing control of itself.**

## **When One Song Becomes an Entire Content Machine**

The World Cup anthem used to stand alone like a monument. Now it is one asset inside a much larger machine.

FIFA has also unveiled the **Official FIFA World Cup 2026 Album**, an 18-track project it describes as the most extensive multi-track music and culture project ever created for the tournament. The track list includes "Dai Dai" alongside songs by artists such as LISA, Anitta, Rema, Future, Tyla, Daddy Yankee, Shenseea, Major Lazer, Nelly Furtado, Davido, Stormzy, Ayra Starr, Latto, Shaggy, and others.

This changes the role of "Dai Dai." It is not simply the song of the tournament; it is the flagship inside a streaming-era ecosystem. It has to live on playlists, sponsor edits, TikTok clips, pre-match packages, fan montages, dance challenges, YouTube compilations, and whatever emotional debris appears after the first penalty shootout.

That can make the World Cup feel more musically diverse. It can also make the official anthem feel less sacred. **When everything is a soundtrack, nothing automatically becomes the song.**

## **The Fine Line Between Global and Generic**

The title "Dai Dai" borrows from Italian, meaning something close to "come on, come on," and WIPO notes that the lyrics move across English, Spanish, Italian, French, and Japanese.

On paper, that is exactly what a 2026 World Cup anthem is supposed to do: travel fast, translate easily, and signal inclusion without requiring explanation. But this is also where World Cup music often collapses into airbrushed universality. Too many anthems reach for everyone and end up belonging to no one. Too many songs about unity sound like they were written by a committee trapped inside a sponsor lounge.

The best World Cup songs avoid that fate by having texture. "The Cup of Life" had theatrical Latin-pop propulsion. "Waka Waka" had bright physicality and a hook that felt both borrowed and reborn. Even unofficial tournament songs can sometimes win the memory war because they feel less polished, less approved, less laminated.

A great World Cup anthem does not need subtlety. It needs friction. It needs sweat. It needs something human enough to survive the institution that commissioned it. **The anthem only becomes real when fans steal it from FIFA.**

## **The Real Test: Can Fans Take It Away From FIFA?**

The real test for **"Dai Dai"** will not be its release campaign. It will not be the official language of culture, unity, and global celebration. It will not even be the first performance, although FIFA has confirmed that Shakira and Burna Boy will bring the song to the World Cup stage at the opening ceremony in Mexico City on **June 11, 2026**.

The real test is much less controllable: **will fans use it when nobody tells them to? **That is where every World Cup anthem either becomes real or dies politely. FIFA can give a song the title. Streaming platforms can give it placement. An official album can surround it with star power; FIFA has already framed the 2026 soundtrack as an **18-track global music project**, the most extensive multi-track music project in World Cup history.  But none of that guarantees emotional ownership.

A World Cup song only starts to matter when it leaves the press release. It has to leak into taxis, bars, schoolyards, fan zones, match-day edits, terrible dance videos, and victory celebrations. It has to be shouted by people who only know half the words. It has to become annoying before it becomes nostalgic.

That is what made **"Waka Waka"** so hard to replace. It did not feel preserved by FIFA; it felt stolen by the public. By the time the tournament ended, the song no longer belonged to the institution that commissioned it. It belonged to everyone who had yelled it badly, danced to it awkwardly, or attached it to a memory they still cannot fully explain.

**"Dai Dai" does not need to beat "Waka Waka." It needs to escape FIFA. **If it stays inside official ceremonies, sponsor reels, and playlist banners, it will be another well-funded tournament song. If fans drag it into their own rituals, it becomes something else: not just the sound of the 2026 World Cup, but proof that the World Cup anthem can still become public property. That is the only memory test that matters.
