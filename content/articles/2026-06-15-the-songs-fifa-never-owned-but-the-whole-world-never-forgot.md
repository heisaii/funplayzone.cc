# The Songs FIFA Never Owned — But The Whole World Never Forgot

*Every four years, FIFA spends millions creating the soundtrack of the World Cup. Every four years, the world picks a different one.*

### How unofficial anthems became the real soundtrack of the World Cup

Every four years, FIFA and its corporate machinery produce an official World Cup anthem. They announce it, stream it, license it, and push it through every available channel. And every four years, a large portion of the planet ignores it completely — and sings something else.

This is a story about those songs. The ones that weren't commissioned, weren't cleared, weren't on the official soundtrack album — but somehow ended up living rent-free in the skulls of billions of people who watched football in the summer heat.

## "Gloryland" Who?

The 1994 World Cup was held in the United States, a country still warming up to the idea that soccer was a real sport. FIFA's official music album for the tournament featured Daryl Hall and Sounds of Blackness performing a song called *Gloryland* — sincere, gospel-tinged, and almost completely invisible in the cultural memory of that World Cup.

**What people actually remember is *****We Will Rock You*****.**

*We Will Rock You* had been released in 1977. By 1994, it was already a fixture at sports arenas worldwide — the kind of song that didn't need an introduction, a marketing budget, or a licensing deal with FIFA. The stomp-stomp-clap pattern was hardwired into the nervous system of anyone who had ever attended a live sporting event. Stadium speakers played it before matches. Fans sang it without being asked. It became, through sheer collective habit, the sonic wallpaper of that entire tournament.

Nobody appointed Queen the official band of USA '94. They just were. *Rolling Stone* would later rank *We Will Rock You* among the 500 greatest songs ever recorded, but it didn't need that validation — the terraces had already made their verdict.

This is what happens when a song is genuinely great and a crowd genuinely wants it: no contract required.

## Pavarotti Changes the Game

Six years earlier, in 1990, a BBC television producer named Philip Bernie had an idea that seemed, by the logic of sports broadcasting, completely insane. For the network's coverage of Italia '90, he wanted to use *Nessun Dorma* — a tenor aria from Puccini's *Turandot*, composed in 1924, never once marketed to football fans — as the opening theme.

The gamble paid off in ways nobody predicted. The aria's final passage, Luciano Pavarotti's voice climbing to that famous *Vincerò!* — "I will win!" — turned out to be one of the most perfectly suited pieces of music ever attached to a sporting event. It captured something the official anthems were always straining for: **genuine emotional stakes.** The sense that something enormous was about to happen.

*Nessun Dorma* hit number two on the UK singles chart. Pavarotti became, improbably, a pop star. The Three Tenors concert — Pavarotti, Plácido Domingo, José Carreras performing together on the eve of the World Cup final at the ancient Baths of Caracalla in Rome — became the best-selling classical album of all time. An opera aria that FIFA had nothing to do with didn't just accompany a World Cup; it defined one.

## "Football's Coming Home" and the Anthem That Keeps Coming Back

In 1996, comedians David Baddiel and Frank Skinner wrote a song with the Lightning Seeds for the European Championship. *Three Lions* was built on the specific bittersweet register of being an England football fan: not quite believing you'll win, unable to stop hoping anyway. "It's coming home," they sang — *football*, the game England invented, finally returning to its birthplace for a tournament.

England didn't win. They rarely do.

But the song came back for the 1998 World Cup in France — and this time, **it beat the official England team song to number one.** The FA-backed release featured the Spice Girls and was the kind of slick, committee-approved product that tends to smell exactly like that. *Three Lions '98*, rawer and funnier and truer, went to the top instead.

It charted again in 2002. Again in 2006. In 2018, when England made the semi-finals for the first time since 1990, it went to number one four separate times — a record in the UK chart's 66-year history, with the same artist lineup each time. No FIFA involvement. No official sanction. Just a song that was so honest about what it felt like to watch England that it kept getting reactivated every time the hope came around again.

## The Coke Ad That Beat the Official Song

The 2010 World Cup in South Africa had an official anthem: Shakira's *Waka Waka (This Time for Africa)*. It was a legitimate banger — Latin pop meets West African rhythm, tailor-made for a tournament on the African continent. FIFA was happy. Sony Music was happy. Shakira was happy.

And yet. K'naan's *Wavin' Flag* — a Coca-Cola promotional track, not an official FIFA product — spent the entire tournament in more people's heads. It had been released in dozens of country-specific versions, including a Mandarin version featuring Jackie Cheung and Jane Zhang that dominated Chinese airwaves. The song's message: — freedom, resilience, the right to exist — resonated in a way that transcended its corporate origins. **The ad song out-emoted the official song.** By the time the tournament ended, a significant portion of the global audience had misremembered it as the real anthem entirely.

## What FIFA Gets Wrong, Every Time

The pattern across these stories is consistent. Official World Cup anthems are products. They're designed in committee, optimized for global markets, road-tested for inoffensiveness, and delivered on a deadline. Sometimes they're genuinely good — *The Cup of Life* by Ricky Martin in 1998 holds up. But they're made to represent the tournament, and there's a ceiling on how much a brand document can move you.

The songs that survive — *Nessun Dorma*, *Three Lions*, *Wavin' Flag*, *We Will Rock You* — weren't made for the World Cup at all. They were made for other reasons, by people with something real to say, and then the World Cup found them. Or fans found them, which amounts to the same thing.

**You can't commission a cultural moment.** You can create conditions for one, or you can stumble into one, but writing a check for it tends to produce exactly the kind of thing people forget by the time the next tournament rolls around.

FIFA owns the official music of the World Cup. The planet owns the soundtrack.
