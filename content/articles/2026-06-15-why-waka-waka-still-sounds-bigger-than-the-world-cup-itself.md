# Why "Waka Waka" Still Sounds Bigger Than the World Cup Itself

*More than a decade after South Africa 2010, Shakira and Freshlyground's "Waka Waka" still feels less like an official football song than a piece of global emotional infrastructure.*

# **The Song That Refused to Stay Official**

There are World Cup songs, and then there is **"Waka Waka (This Time for Africa)"**—a song so permanently welded to football memory that it no longer feels like it belongs to one tournament, one host nation, or even FIFA itself.

Officially, it was the song of the **2010 FIFA World Cup in South Africa**, performed by Shakira with the South African band Freshlyground. FIFA identifies it as the official song of South Africa 2010 and notes that Shakira performed it with Freshlyground during the closing ceremony at Soccer City in Johannesburg.

But "official" is the least interesting thing about it. Plenty of official World Cup songs have arrived with bigger rollouts, louder branding, and more obvious committee logic. Most of them evaporate the second the trophy is lifted.

**"Waka Waka" did the opposite. It escaped.**

It escaped the closing ceremony. It escaped South Africa 2010. It escaped the four-year tournament cycle. t became dance-routine material, YouTube nostalgia, football edits, karaoke fuel, and the kind of song people claim to be tired of right before singing every syllable they know.

Guinness World Records lists "Waka Waka" as the **most-streamed FIFA World Cup song on Spotify**, with **1,074,458,645 streams** counted by January 13, 2025. Its official video had also passed **4 billion views** on YouTube.

Those numbers are enormous. They are also not the point.

**The real achievement of "Waka Waka" is that it turned marketing into memory.**

# **A Hook Too Physical to Be Contained**

The genius of "Waka Waka" is not subtlety. World Cup songs should never be subtle. Subtlety dies in a stadium. Subtlety cannot survive 70,000 people, plastic horns, beer, tears, nationalism, and someone screaming the wrong words into the night.

What "Waka Waka" understands is that a football anthem needs to be physical before it is clever. Its hook does not ask to be admired; it asks to be repeated. The rhythm is bright, immediate, and almost childlike in its confidence. The song does not build a cathedral. It builds a crowd.

That is why it still works on terrible speakers. That is why it survives tinny phone videos, low-resolution highlight reels, and the violent compression of social media uploads. Some songs lose their body when they leave the studio. "Waka Waka" seems to have gained one.

**It is not a song you listen to politely. It is a song that makes politeness impossible.**

Shakira's vocal performance is central to that effect. She does not sing it like a guest at a corporate event. She attacks it with the strange certainty of someone who knows the hook has already won. Her voice is bright but not weightless, playful but not disposable. She makes the song feel athletic without becoming macho, joyous without becoming empty.

That balance is rare. Many World Cup songs confuse size with shouting. "Waka Waka" understands that the biggest sound in a stadium is not volume. It is permission.

# **It Was Never Just Shakira's Song**

The mythology around "Waka Waka" often turns it into a Shakira story, which is understandable but incomplete.

Freshlyground matter. Their presence gives the track texture, place, and warmth. FIFA describes the band as a Cape Town Afro-fusion group, and their role helps keep the song from sounding like a global-pop superstar simply parachuting into a host continent.

That tension matters because "Waka Waka" was not born without controversy. WIPO notes that FIFA's choice of a Colombian superstar for the first World Cup hosted on the African continent drew protests demanding African representation. The same account also notes that South African band Freshlyground and local artists were brought into the wider musical picture around the song and ceremonies.

This history complicates the triumph of "Waka Waka," and it should. The song's greatness does not erase the awkward politics of global pop, where "world music" can easily become a costume worn by institutions with enormous budgets and selective memory.

But that complication is also part of why the song remains fascinating. "Waka Waka" is not pure. It is not innocent. It is a product of FIFA, Sony, celebrity power, cultural borrowing, African pop history, Latin pop instinct, and a global audience ready to turn one chorus into a ritual.

**Its power comes from the fact that it sounds communal even when its creation story is messy.**

# **The Chorus Had a Life Before the World Cup**

Part of why "Waka Waka" feels older than 2010 is because, in a real sense, it is.

WIPO notes that the song faced plagiarism claims over its chorus, which was linked to **"Zangaléwa,"** a 1980s hit by Cameroonian group Golden Sounds. According to WIPO, Sony Music and Shakira's management reached an out-of-court settlement with the Zangaléwa writers, who were then credited as co-writers on "Waka Waka."

That background is essential because it explains something a streaming total never could: "Waka Waka" did not feel invented in a lab. It felt like something that had already been moving through bodies before FIFA arrived.

This does not make the song less complicated. It makes it more interesting. The chorus carries the strange force of something borrowed, adapted, disputed, repackaged, and then released into a tournament that wanted the entire planet to feel united for a month.

A weaker song would collapse under that contradiction. "Waka Waka" turned contradiction into momentum.

**It sounded new to millions because it was already old enough to feel familiar.**

# **Why Every Later World Cup Song Sounds Like an Attempt**

After "Waka Waka," the World Cup anthem became a haunted category.

Every new official song has to answer the same impossible question: can it become this big? Can it travel this far? Can it be annoying in the present and sacred in retrospect? Can it become both a meme and a memory?

Most cannot.

That is not because later songs lacked stars. FIFA has repeatedly recruited huge names, from Pitbull, Jennifer Lopez, and Claudia Leitte in 2014 to Nicky Jam, Will Smith, and Era Istrefi in 2018. FIFA's own 2018 release places those songs within the official-song lineage that includes "Waka Waka."

The problem is not star power. The problem is ownership.

Too many tournament songs sound like they are begging the world to feel united. "Waka Waka" simply behaves as if the world already is. It does not over-explain its emotional function. It does not keep pointing at its own importance. It trusts rhythm more than slogans.

That is why it still dwarfs the category. It has the rare confidence of a song that does not seem afraid of being obvious.

**"Waka Waka" is not better because it is more sophisticated. It is better because it knows exactly how unsophisticated collective joy needs to be.**

# **The 2026 Problem: Shakira Is Now Competing With Herself**

This is what makes Shakira's return for 2026 so loaded.

FIFA has announced **"Dai Dai,"** Shakira's collaboration with Burna Boy, as the **Official FIFA World Cup 2026 Song**, with royalties supporting the FIFA Global Citizen Education Fund.  On paper, that is a brilliant move: bring back the one artist most associated with the modern World Cup anthem and pair her with one of the defining global voices of African pop.

But the shadow is brutal.

"Dai Dai" is not only being judged as a 2026 song. It is being judged against the ghost of 2010, against a version of the internet that no longer exists, against a World Cup that felt more singular, and against a chorus that has had more than a decade to harden into myth.

That is unfair. It is also unavoidable.

**Shakira gave FIFA its most immortal modern anthem. Now every Shakira World Cup song has to survive being compared to Shakira.**

# **The Memory Is Bigger Than the Match**

What makes "Waka Waka" still sound bigger than the World Cup itself is that it no longer needs football to function.

The song can return during any tournament, any fan montage, any summer party, any nostalgic scroll through old clips. It does not require the context of Spain winning in 2010, or South Africa hosting, or a specific match, goal, or ceremony. It carries the tournament feeling without needing the tournament facts.

That is the highest form of World Cup anthem success. Not accuracy. Not prestige. Not even popularity, exactly.

Transformation.

A great World Cup song starts as a soundtrack for football and becomes a storage device for emotion. "Waka Waka" stores joy, embarrassment, childhood, nationalism, movement, memory, and the strange collective delusion that billions of people might briefly belong to the same chorus.

This is why it has outlived so many more expensive songs. It does not sound like a campaign. It sounds like the campaign accidentally opened a door and let real life in.

**"Waka Waka" still sounds bigger than the World Cup because it became what the World Cup keeps promising to be: loud, messy, global, sentimental, commercial, ridiculous, and somehow real.**

For three and a half minutes, it makes the world feel choreographed.

That is not just a good football anthem. That is pop music doing the one thing football has always claimed it can do: make strangers move together before they remember all the reasons they are supposed to stand apart.
